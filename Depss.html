Chapter One: Introduction

1.1 Background of the Study

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) is a clinical syndrome characterized by the ascending spread of microorganisms from the lower reproductive tract specifically the vagina and cervix to the upper female reproductive organs, including the uterus (endometritis), fallopian tubes (salpingitis), ovaries (oophoritis), and the surrounding pelvic peritoneum. As a leading cause of preventable reproductive morbidity, PID is globally recognized for its long-term complications, which include chronic pelvic pain, tubal infertility, and an increased risk of ectopic pregnancy. The etiology of PID is characteristically polymicrobial, involving a complex interplay between sexually transmitted pathogens, such as Chlamydia trachomatis and Neisseria gonorrhoeae, and a diverse range of endogenous vaginal flora, including Escherichia coli, Staphylococcus aureus, Klebsiella species, and various anaerobic bacteria that ascend to the upper tract when the natural vaginal balance is disrupted (Akinyemi et al., 2020).

The clinical presentation of PID is highly variable, ranging from subclinical or "silent" cases where patients exhibit no obvious symptoms, allowing the infection to cause progressive, irreversible damage, to acute, symptomatic presentations (Nwadioha S.I. et al., 2020). Typical symptoms include lower abdominal or pelvic pain, abnormal, often foul-smelling vaginal discharge, dyspareunia (pain during intercourse), dysuria (painful urination), and menstrual irregularities. In advanced cases, systemic responses such as fever, chills, nausea, and vomiting may manifest, often signaling the development of complications like a tubo-ovarian abscess. Because these symptoms can overlap with other gynecological conditions, PID is often classified as acute, chronic, or subclinical, with the subclinical form posing the greatest challenge to early diagnosis and public health intervention (Ogeni A, 2016).

The susceptibility of university students to PID is driven by an intersection of behavioral, environmental, and biological risk factors. Behavioral risks, such as early sexual debut, inconsistent use of barrier protection, and having multiple or concurrent sexual partners, significantly elevate the probability of acquiring infection. Environmental factors, particularly communal living conditions in university hostels, can also play a role by limiting access to private hygiene facilities and potentially facilitating the spread of infections. Furthermore, biological factors such as young age, specifically women under 25, render individuals more susceptible due to anatomical and physiological cervical characteristics that are more prone to infection. Other contributors, including the historical occurrence of sexually transmitted infections (STIs), previous episodes of PID, and practices such as vaginal douching, further exacerbate these risks by disrupting the protective vaginal microbiota and facilitating the upward migration of bacteria (Nwadioha S.I. et al., 2020).

Given the polymicrobial nature of PID and the global rise in antimicrobial resistance, the use of antibiograms has become essential for effective clinical management. An antibiogram is a laboratory-derived summary of the antimicrobial susceptibility of isolated pathogens, providing a critical diagnostic map for choosing the most effective therapeutic agents. Relying on generic or outdated treatment protocols often results in therapeutic failure, particularly as common pathogens like S. aureus and E. coli show increasing resistance to first-line beta-lactam antibiotics like amoxicillin and penicillin (Akinyemi et al., 2020).

Consequently, localized antibiogram data is vital for antibiotic stewardship, which prioritizes the use of targeted, evidence-based therapy to ensure optimal patient recovery and curb the development of multidrug-resistant (MDR) strains. By generating a contemporary profile of bacterial susceptibility, this research provides the necessary clinical decision support to move beyond empirical guessing, ensuring that medical interventions are localized, effective, and sustainable for the long-term reproductive health of the student population (Ogeni A, 2016).

1.2 Justification

This study is justified by the increasing failure of standard PID treatment protocols in rural clinics. By analyzing both urine and HVS samples, this research provides a dual perspective on the urogenital health of the patients, acknowledging that PID and urinary tract infections (UTIs) often coexist or mimic one another.

Providing a current antibiogram will:

  • Reduce the time to recovery by ensuring the right antibiotic is used from the start.
  • Help curb the development of further resistance by discouraging the use of ineffective drugs.
  • Provide economic benefit by reducing the financial burden on rural patients who currently pay for multiple rounds of ineffective trial treatments.

1.3 Aim

The aim is to evaluate the antibiogram of bacterial isolates from urine and HVS samples of PID patients in Evangel University.

Objectives

  • To determine the antimicrobial susceptibility patterns (antibiogram) of the isolates against a panel of commonly available antibiotics (e.g., ciprofloxacin, ceftriaxone, gentamicin, augmentin).
  • To develop an antibiotic policy for the treatment of PID in Evangel University Clinic.

Chapter Two: Literature Review

2.1 Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) is a major gynecological condition that involves infection and inflammation of the female upper genital tract, including the uterus, fallopian tubes, and ovaries. It is most commonly associated with untreated sexually transmitted infections (STIs), particularly Neisseria gonorrhoeae and Chlamydia trachomatis. However, recent studies emphasize that PID is polymicrobial, involving both aerobic and anaerobic bacteria, which complicates treatment and increases the risk of chronic sequelae (Brunham et al., 2015).

Globally, PID affects millions of women annually, with higher prevalence in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia. According to the World Health Organization, PID contributes to infertility in up to 30% of women in developing countries, making it not only a medical issue but also a socioeconomic challenge. Clinically, PID presents with lower abdominal pain, abnormal vaginal discharge, fever, and dyspareunia. However, asymptomatic cases are common, complicating diagnosis and leading to delayed treatment. Laboratory investigations, including urine and high vaginal swab (HVS) cultures, are essential for identifying causative organisms and guiding therapy (Ross et al., 2007).

2.2 Types of Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

PID can be classified into several types based on severity, clinical presentation, and complications. The main types include acute PID, chronic PID, subclinical PID, recurrent PID, and complicated PID such as tubo-ovarian abscess.

Acute PID: Presents with sudden onset of symptoms such as severe lower abdominal pain, fever, abnormal vaginal discharge, and tenderness on pelvic examination. It is often associated with Neisseria gonorrhoeae or Chlamydia trachomatis infections. If untreated, acute PID can progress rapidly to complications like tubo-ovarian abscess or peritonitis.

Chronic PID: Develops when acute infection is inadequately treated or persists over time. Symptoms include long-standing pelvic pain, irregular menstruation, and infertility. Chronic inflammation leads to scarring and adhesions in the fallopian tubes, increasing the risk of ectopic pregnancy.

Subclinical PID: Is asymptomatic or presents with very mild symptoms that may go unnoticed. Despite the absence of overt signs, it causes endometrial and tubal inflammation detectable through biopsy or imaging. Subclinical PID is strongly linked to infertility because damage occurs silently over time.

Recurrent PID: Occurs when patients experience repeated episodes of infection. It is common in women with multiple sexual partners or untreated sexually transmitted infections. Each recurrence increases the risk of infertility and chronic pelvic pain.

Complicated PID: Refers to cases where infection leads to abscess formation, peritonitis, or systemic involvement. Tubo-ovarian abscess (TOA) is the most common complication, presenting with severe pain, fever, and adnexal mass. These cases often require hospitalization, intravenous antibiotics, and sometimes surgical intervention.

2.3 The Pathology of PID

In Nigeria and other sub-Saharan African countries, PID remains a leading cause of gynecological morbidity. Poor access to diagnostic facilities and indiscriminate antibiotic use exacerbate the problem. Continuous surveillance and research into its microbial etiology are therefore essential (Spencer et al., 2014).

PID begins when microorganisms from the vagina or cervix ascend into the normally sterile upper genital tract. The endocervical canal acts as a protective barrier, but infections with sexually transmitted pathogens such as Neisseria gonorrhoeae and Chlamydia trachomatis disrupt this defense. Once compromised, vaginal flora including anaerobes and facultative bacteria gain access to the endometrium, fallopian tubes, and ovaries, initiating inflammation and tissue damage. This ascending infection is the hallmark of PID pathogenesis (Ross, Marrazzo, & Bloom, 2025).

Mycoplasma genitalium has emerged as an important pathogen in PID. Its ability to adhere to epithelial cells and evade immune clearance allows persistent infection. Studies show strong associations between M. genitalium and endometritis, salpingitis, and infertility. Unlike gonorrhea and chlamydia, M. genitalium infections are often asymptomatic, making them difficult to detect and treat (Lis, Rowhani-Rahbar, & Manhart, 2015).

Host immune responses play a dual role in PID. While neutrophils and cytokines attempt to eliminate pathogens, they also cause collateral tissue damage. Chronic inflammation leads to scarring, adhesions, and tubal obstruction. This immunopathology explains why PID often results in infertility and ectopic pregnancy even after microbial clearance (Taylor, Darville, & Haggerty, 2017).

Polymicrobial synergy is another hallmark of PID pathogenesis. Anaerobes such as Bacteroides fragilis and Peptostreptococcus thrive in the altered environment created by STI pathogens. Their presence contributes to abscess formation and more severe disease outcomes. This explains why broad-spectrum antibiotics are required for effective PID treatment (Manhart & Broad, 2020).

Hormonal influences also modulate PID risk. Estrogen and progesterone affect cervical mucus viscosity and immune responses, altering susceptibility to infection. Oral contraceptives may reduce PID risk by thickening cervical mucus, but they can also change vaginal flora composition, influencing microbial ascent (Vodstrcil et al., 2017).

Recent genomic studies reveal that PID involves complex host-pathogen interactions. Pathogens trigger epithelial cell apoptosis and disrupt tight junctions, facilitating microbial invasion. Meanwhile, host genetic factors, such as polymorphisms in immune-related genes, influence susceptibility and severity of PID (Haggerty et al., 2016).

Co-infections exacerbate PID severity. Women with concurrent gonorrhea and chlamydia infections experience more extensive tissue damage and higher rates of infertility. This synergistic effect underscores the importance of comprehensive STI screening in PID prevention (Toth et al., 2019).

The spread of PID beyond the pelvis can result in systemic complications. Fitz-Hugh–Curtis syndrome, characterized by perihepatic adhesions, is a classic example. This demonstrates how PID pathogenesis is not confined to the reproductive tract but can extend to distant organs, increasing morbidity (Workowski & Bachmann, 2021).

Complications of PID are severe and include tubal factor infertility, ectopic pregnancy, and chronic pelvic pain. These sequelae highlight the importance of early diagnosis and effective treatment. Antibiogram studies of isolates from urine and HVS samples provide critical guidance for therapy (Simms et al., 2006).

2.4 Bacterial Isolates from Urine and High Vaginal Swabs (HVS)

Urine and HVS samples are indispensable in identifying pathogens associated with PID. These specimens provide direct evidence of microbial colonization and infection in the genitourinary tract. Studies consistently show that both Gram-positive and Gram-negative organisms are implicated, reflecting the polymicrobial nature of PID.

  • Escherichia coli: frequently isolated from urine samples of PID patients. Its pathogenicity is linked to virulence factors such as adhesins and toxins, which facilitate colonization and tissue damage. Resistance to beta-lactams and fluoroquinolones is increasingly reported.
  • Staphylococcus aureus: another common isolate, particularly from HVS samples. Its ability to produce toxins and biofilms enhances persistence in the genital tract. Methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA) strains pose significant therapeutic challenges.
  • Klebsiella pneumoniae and Proteus mirabilis: Gram-negative organisms often implicated in urinary tract involvement in PID. Their urease activity contributes to urinary alkalinization and stone formation.
  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa: less frequently isolated but clinically significant due to its intrinsic resistance mechanisms. Its presence often indicates chronic or recurrent infections.
  • Anaerobic bacteria such as Bacteroides fragilis and Peptostreptococcus are also recovered from HVS samples. They contribute to the polymicrobial nature of PID and are associated with severe complications like tubo-ovarian abscesses.

The diversity of isolates underscores the importance of comprehensive microbiological analysis. Empirical therapy without culture and sensitivity testing risks treatment failure and resistance development.

2.5 Diagnostic Approach of Pelvic Inflammatory Disease

The diagnosis of PID remains challenging because of its polymicrobial etiology and variable clinical presentation. Classical symptoms such as lower abdominal pain, abnormal vaginal discharge, and cervical motion tenderness are often nonspecific, overlapping with other gynecological and gastrointestinal conditions. PID is considered a syndrome diagnosis, integrating clinical findings, laboratory results, and imaging studies rather than relying on a single test.

Clinical evaluation remains the cornerstone of PID diagnosis. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) recommend empiric treatment in sexually active women presenting with pelvic pain if no other cause is identified and if cervical motion, uterine, or adnexal tenderness is present. However, clinical diagnosis alone has limited sensitivity and specificity, underscoring the need for adjunctive diagnostic tools.

Laboratory testing is critical in confirming PID. Endocervical swabs and urine samples are routinely tested for Neisseria gonorrhoeae and Chlamydia trachomatis using nucleic acid amplification tests (NAATs), which are highly sensitive and specific. Recent advances also allow detection of Mycoplasma genitalium, an emerging pathogen strongly associated with PID. These tests provide microbiological evidence that complements clinical findings.

Inflammatory markers such as C-reactive protein (CRP), erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), and white blood cell count are often elevated in PID, but they lack specificity. Recent studies suggest that procalcitonin may serve as a more reliable biomarker for severe PID, particularly in cases complicated by tubo-ovarian abscess. These markers help clinicians assess disease severity and guide treatment decisions.

Imaging techniques are increasingly used to support PID diagnosis. Transvaginal ultrasound can detect adnexal masses, free pelvic fluid, and thickened fallopian tubes, which are suggestive of PID. However, ultrasound findings may be subtle in early disease. Magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) offers greater sensitivity, particularly for identifying tubo-ovarian abscesses, but is less accessible in low resource settings.

Laparoscopy remains the gold standard for PID diagnosis, allowing direct visualization of inflamed pelvic organs and collection of samples for culture. However, its invasive nature and limited availability restrict its use to severe or uncertain cases. Recent guidelines recommend laparoscopy primarily for women with suspected tubo-ovarian abscess or when differential diagnosis is unclear (Haggerty & Taylor, 2017).

Molecular diagnostics have revolutionized PID detection. NAATs not only identify classical pathogens but also detect emerging organisms such as Mycoplasma genitalium and Ureaplasma urealyticum. Multiplex PCR assays allow simultaneous detection of multiple pathogens, improving diagnostic accuracy. These innovations are particularly valuable in cases of subclinical PID, where symptoms are minimal but reproductive damage is ongoing (Unemo & Jensen, 2017).

Point-of-care testing is gaining attention as a diagnostic approach for PID, especially in resource-limited settings. Rapid tests for gonorrhea and chlamydia allow immediate treatment decisions, reducing delays that contribute to complications. Although these tests are less sensitive than laboratory NAATs, their accessibility makes them valuable in high burden regions (Van Der Pol, 2020).

Despite advances, PID diagnosis remains imperfect. Many women with subclinical PID are missed because they lack overt symptoms. This silent progression contributes significantly to infertility rates worldwide. Recent research emphasizes the need for improved screening strategies, including routine STI testing and microbiome analysis, to identify women at risk before irreversible damage occurs (Taylor et al., 2018).

The diagnostic approach to PID requires a multimodal strategy combining clinical evaluation, laboratory testing, imaging, and, in select cases, laparoscopy. Advances in molecular diagnostics and point-of-care testing are improving detection, but challenges remain, particularly in identifying subclinical disease. A comprehensive diagnostic framework is essential to prevent long-term complications such as infertility and ectopic pregnancy (Ross et al., 2017).

2.6 Antibiogram Patterns in Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

The management of Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID) has become increasingly complex due to the global escalation of antimicrobial resistance (AMR). An antibiogram serves as a systematic laboratory report detailing the susceptibility of isolated microorganisms to various antimicrobial agents. In the context of PID, the antibiogram is a critical diagnostic tool used to transition from empirical therapy to targeted, evidence-based treatment (Akinyemi et al., 2020).

2.7 The Rationale for Local Antibiogram Surveillance

The reliance on generic treatment protocols often results in therapeutic failure, as resistance profiles are both geographically and temporally dynamic. Recent clinical research has underscored that resistance patterns observed in urban tertiary hospitals may not accurately reflect the ecology of pathogens found in university or hostel-based settings (Nwadioha et al., 2020). By conducting localized antibiogram surveillance, clinicians can implement antibiotic stewardship, a framework that prioritizes the use of the most efficacious narrow-spectrum antibiotics to achieve patient recovery while minimizing the risk of drug-induced adverse effects and the selection of multidrug-resistant strains (Oseni, 2016).

2.8 Trends in Bacterial Resistance

A review of recent literature indicates a significant shift in the susceptibility profiles of common PID-associated pathogens. Studies have consistently reported a declining efficacy of traditional first-line therapies. High rates of resistance to conventional beta-lactam antibiotics such as ampicillin, amoxicillin, and penicillin have been documented across multiple Nigerian clinical studies (Raimi & Ochayi, 2017; Akinyemi et al., 2020). This widespread resistance is largely attributed to the indiscriminate over-the-counter availability of these antibiotics and high rates of self-medication among the student population, which facilitates the horizontal transfer of resistance genes among pathogens like Staphylococcus aureus and Escherichia coli (Nwadioha et al., 2020).

Conversely, studies have demonstrated that newer-generation agents, particularly third-generation cephalosporins (e.g., ceftriaxone, cefotaxime) and fluoroquinolones (e.g., ofloxacin, ciprofloxacin), currently maintain higher sensitivity profiles against common Gram-negative and Gram-positive isolates (Oseni, 2016). Furthermore, aminoglycosides like gentamicin remain highly effective in local clinical settings, often serving as a reliable alternative when beta-lactam resistance is confirmed (Raimi & Ochayi, 2017). However, these patterns are not static; resistance to these newer agents is already emerging in many environments, necessitating continuous monitoring and updating of clinical guidelines (Nwadioha et al., 2020).

Chapter Three

3.1 Study Area

The study was conducted at Evangel University, Akaeze, Ebonyi State, Nigeria. The clinic serves students, staff, and members of the surrounding community, providing primary healthcare services.

3.2 Study Design

A cross-sectional descriptive study design was employed. It aimed at analyzing the antibiogram patterns of bacterial isolates obtained from women with suspected PID.

3.3 Study Population

The study population consisted of female patients attending Evangel University Akaeze who presented with symptoms suggestive of pelvic inflammatory disease. These patients were expected to be within reproductive age (18–35 years), as PID is most prevalent in this group.

3.4 Selection Criteria

Inclusion Criteria:

  • Female patients clinically diagnosed with PID.
  • Patients who consent to participate in the study.
  • Patients within the reproductive age group (18–35 years).

Exclusion Criteria:

  • Patients currently on antibiotic therapy.
  • Patients unwilling to provide samples or consent.
  • Male patients and females outside the reproductive age group.

3.5 Sample Size

The sample size consisted of 60 bacterial isolates collected from suspected PID patients.

3.6 Materials

  • Biochemical test reagents: indole, coagulase, catalase
  • Antibiotic discs (ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, ceftriaxone, tetracycline, etc.)
  • Incubator (set at 37°C)
  • Microscope and Gram staining reagents
  • Personal protective equipment (gloves, lab coats, masks)

3.7 Method

  • Sample collection: A total of 60 bacterial isolates were collected from the laboratory where the urine and HVS samples of suspected PID patients were processed.
  • Confirmatory biochemical tests: Bacterial colonies were confirmed by Gram staining and biochemical tests (e.g., catalase, coagulase, indole tests).
  • Antibiotic Susceptibility Testing (AST): The Kirby-Bauer disc diffusion method was employed following Clinical and Laboratory Standards Institute (CLSI) guidelines.
  • Common antibiotics such as ciprofloxacin, gentamicin, ceftriaxone, amoxicillin-clavulanate, and tetracycline were tested.
  • Zones of inhibition were measured and interpreted as sensitive, intermediate, or resistant.

Data analysis: Data were analyzed using Statistical Package for the Social Sciences (SPSS) and the susceptibility pattern of bacterial isolates to different antibiotics was determined. Results were categorized as sensitive or resistant. The percentage resistance of each organism to each antibiotic was calculated. Inferential statistics like the Chi-square test were used to determine the association between type of bacterial isolate and antibiotic resistance.
